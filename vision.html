The App That Stays
With Her

A vision for a faith-based, natural-minded daily companion — for pregnancy, birth, and the season nobody talks about after.

Prepared by Ava Sinclaire · Higney International · Confidential

The Problem

Every App Abandons Her

There are over twenty pregnancy apps on the market. They are downloaded millions of times a year. And almost every single one does the same thing: they dump information on her, push her into forums with 80,000 strangers, and then — the moment her baby is born — they disappear.

The apps that don't disappear pivot to tracking the baby. Feeds. Diapers. Sleep schedules. Which is useful. But nobody asks how she is doing. Nobody gives her a simple guide for Day 3 postpartum, when her milk is coming in and she's crying for reasons she can't explain and her body feels like a stranger's.

"During pregnancy, we're cared for and monitored. But once we give birth, it's like the world forgets us."

— Reddit, r/beyondthebump (one of thousands of similar posts)

There's a second problem: the women who are most underserved aren't the ones without options. They're the ones with values. The natural-minded mom who researches everything, who's making intentional choices about birth and feeding and what she puts in her body. The faith-based mom who prays over her pregnancy and wants an app that acknowledges God's design for this experience — not one that treats her faith like a quirky sidebar.

These women have no app. They piece together Instagram accounts, email newsletters, Facebook groups, and books. Something better is long overdue.

Identity

What We'd Call It

The name can't sound clinical. Not "PregnancyTrack Pro." Not "MomWell." Something warm, intimate, personal — the kind of name she'd whisper to a friend, not see on a medical brochure.

Rooted. As in rooted in faith. Rooted in her body. Rooted in who she's becoming as a mother. Or Tend — the act of caring for something precious and growing. The name matters because this woman chooses her brands the same way she chooses her church and her midwife: with her whole self.

"You are not a patient. You are a woman doing the most sacred, demanding, transformative thing a human being can do. You deserve a companion — not a manual."

— The core philosophy this app is built on
Core Experience

The Home Screen Is Called "Today"

The single most important insight from our research: moms want to know what to do today. Not forty articles. Not a forum full of strangers. Just — open the app, see my day, do the things, close it.

Every competitor has missed this. Every single one. They've built information archives when what women are begging for is a simple daily guide.

When she opens the app, she sees her name, her week, a single gentle line about where her baby is right now. And then — her day:

Today
Good morning, Shameen 🌅
Week 31, Day 4
Baby weighs ~3.3 lbs · 🥥 Coconut
✓
Prenatal vitamin
80oz water today
Protein goal — 75g
Pelvic tilts · Hip circles
🙏 Today's Prayer
"Lord, as my body works harder each day, remind me that I was made for this. Give me rest that restores, not just sleep that passes time."

That's it. Five things and a prayer. She's done in 60 seconds — or she lingers. Her choice. No algorithm dragging her deeper. No ads. No drama. Just her day.

Today's Prayer & Verse — Written for Her Week

This is the feature that will get screenshotted and texted to friends more than anything else in the app. Not because it's innovative — because it's specific.

Week 31 brings exhaustion, Braxton Hicks, the nesting instinct, and the first real anxiety about labor. The prayer should speak to that — not offer a generic "bless this pregnancy" placeholder. Isaiah 40:31. Psalm 139. Written like a friend who actually knows what week 31 feels like.

Day 3 Postpartum Prayer (example)

"God, I am undone. And I think that might be okay. You put yourself into a vulnerable body too. Hold me here."

She will screenshot this at 3am and text it to her mom. That's word-of-mouth that no ad budget can buy.

App Structure

Four Spaces

1. Today — The Daily Guide

The home screen. Daily actions, prayer, verse, encouragement. Reviewed in 60 seconds. This is where she opens every morning.

2. My Journey — Her Personal Record

Weekly reflection prompts. Symptom log. Mood check-ins — not a clinical PHQ-9 form, just: how are you feeling, really? A beautiful scrollable timeline she can look back on. This becomes her pregnancy memoir without her realizing it.

3. The Guide — Content Library

Information lives here — but she comes to it when she wants to, not because the app shoves articles at her. Three key sections:

  • Birth Your Way — hospital, birth center, and homebirth covered with equal depth and zero judgment
  • Natural Living — elimination communication, cloth diapering, babywearing, herbal pregnancy support, non-toxic home, clean feeding
  • Faith & Motherhood — devotionals, testimonies from believing moms, the sacred narrative of birth (not just the pain-as-curse narrative)

4. Circle — Her Community

This is not a forum. This is not 80,000 strangers. She gets placed in a group of 30–50 women due the same month, with similar values — natural-minded, faith-based. That's her Circle.

  • One conversation prompt per day — not an open feed
  • Prayer request wall — anonymous posting, others pray without responding
  • Milestone celebrations — when someone's baby arrives, the whole Circle celebrates
  • After birth, the Circle transitions together into postpartum — same women, same bonds, now navigating new motherhood side by side

Why Circle Works

BabyCenter's forums have 80,000 members per birth month and are rated 1.8 stars for toxicity. Peanut — the most loved community app — works because it's small and intentional. A values-aligned Circle of 30–50 women is not a feature. It becomes the reason she never deletes the app.

The Differentiator

Postpartum — Where This App Earns Its Stripes

This is the part no other app does. This is the moat.

The moment she enters her birth date, the app transforms. Not a hard pivot — a gentle transition. The pregnancy tracker doesn't just shut off. It honors what just happened:

The Transition Screen

"Baby [Name] was born on [Date]. You did something extraordinary. Now let's take care of you."

The postpartum home screen is still called "Today" — but the content shifts entirely to her recovery. Day 3 might look like this:

Today · Postpartum
Day 3 — You're doing it 💛
Your milk is likely coming in. Your body is doing something remarkable and uncomfortable at the same time.
Water — 100oz (breastfeeding)
Eat something warm + nourishing
Peri bottle rinse ×3
Rest — lie down, even briefly
Let someone else hold baby 20 min

"What's Normal" — The Feature Nobody Built

Week by week, day by day: what is normal right now, and what isn't. Not as a clinical checklist — as honest, compassionate information delivered like a doula friend who's seen it all.

"Week 1: Heavy bleeding is normal. Large clots (golf ball size or larger) are not — call your provider. Night sweats are normal. Feeling weepy and overwhelmed is normal. Feeling nothing is also normal."

She will read this at 3am, alone, scared. And she will feel less alone. That is the entire purpose of this feature.

Postpartum Daily Guide: 90 Days + First Year

The guide runs day-by-day for the first 90 days — then weekly through baby's first year. Baby tracking exists, but it is not the center. The app's opening question is always about her, not the baby. Baby gets a card. Mom gets the whole screen.

"We're expected to recover and bounce back as if nothing happened. I don't know how my body is supposed to feel."

— Reddit, r/postpartumprogress
Core DNA

Faith Woven In — Not Bolted On

The easiest mistake to make: treating faith like a feature. A toggle. "Turn on Christian mode." That's not it.

Faith should be the DNA of how the app speaks. The language. The framing of birth as sacred, not merely medical. The acknowledgment that this baby was prayed for. That her body is not a machine — it's a temple doing holy work.

What That Looks Like in Practice

  • Onboarding asks about faith background and adjusts the entire tone of the app — not just swapping one word
  • Week 20 anatomy scan: "Today you might see the face God has been designing. Psalm 139:13–14. Go in with open hands."
  • Postpartum depression is addressed with both practical guidance and spiritual honesty: "The darkness after birth is real. It is not a spiritual failure. The Psalms are full of people in exactly this place."
  • The prayer wall in Circle is the most-used feature — faith-based community apps consistently find prayer touchpoints drive the highest engagement
  • Content never condescends or qualifies faith. It simply speaks from it.
Content Philosophy

Natural/Crunchy as the Default — Not the Exception

The app speaks natural by default. If she wants information about conventional options, it's available — but this app doesn't center it.

Content That Exists Nowhere Else

  • Elimination Communication (EC) — a full, proper guide based on Andrea Olson's framework. The only app to ever cover this.
  • Babywearing — carries, wraps, safety, benefits — integrated as a standard parenting approach
  • Natural birth preparation — hypnobirthing, breathing, positions, water birth, what to expect in each birth setting
  • Traditional postpartum healing — the 40-day rest, bone broth, belly binding, sitz baths — featured as wisdom that predates modern medicine, not as "alternative"
  • Clean living through pregnancy — non-toxic nursery, organic food guidance, what to avoid

On Vaccines

The app says nothing. Literally nothing. No CDC schedule. No "consult your pediatrician about vaccines." Complete silence. She can look that up elsewhere. This app is not touching it — and that silence will speak louder to her than anything else. She'll notice. She'll trust.

Growth

Why She Tells Her Friends

Word-of-mouth is everything in this community. Here's what gets shared:

  • The prayers. She screenshots them. She texts them to her mom, her sister, her best friend who's also pregnant. These go viral within friend groups — no ad budget required.
  • "What's Normal." She reads it at 3am, scared and alone. It calms her down. She immediately shares it with her postpartum group chat.
  • Her Circle. "I have this group of 30 moms all due in May — we pray for each other and they just get it." That's not an app feature. That's a relationship. She'll talk about it for years.
  • The birth plan section. She shows her husband what she built. He finally understands what she wants from birth. Her doula asks what app it's from. Her OB asks to see it.
